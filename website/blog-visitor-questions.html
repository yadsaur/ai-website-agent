The 100 Questions Visitors Ask Before Buying

Most buying questions look different on the surface, but they usually belong to a small number of intent patterns. Once you see the pattern, you understand how the website should answer.

People rarely ask random questions when they are close to buying. They ask versions of the same questions over and over: What does this do? How much does it cost? Can I trust it? Will it work for me? How do I get started? What happens after I sign up? The exact words change, but the intent behind them is surprisingly consistent.

That matters because a useful website chatbot should not answer every question in the same tone or structure. A pricing question needs a direct answer. A trust question needs specifics. A setup question needs a next step. A navigation question needs a precise location. If everything gets turned into one flat summary style, the chatbot sounds generic even when the content is technically relevant.

The six categories that matter most

We can group most pre-purchase questions into a handful of categories that are easy to recognize once you start looking for them.

  • Pricing and billing questions.
  • Product understanding and fit questions.
  • Trust and legitimacy questions.
  • Security and compliance questions.
  • Support and onboarding questions.
  • Getting-started and “where do I click?” questions.

Each of these has a different emotional job. Pricing reduces uncertainty about cost. Product understanding reduces ambiguity about fit. Trust reduces perceived risk. Security reduces organizational hesitation. Support reduces fear of getting stuck. Navigation reduces friction at the exact moment a visitor wants to act.

Why pricing and onboarding questions are especially valuable

Not all questions signal the same level of intent. Someone asking “What does this product do?” may still be early in evaluation. Someone asking “How much does it cost?” or “How do I sign up?” is much closer to a decision. These are the moments where poor answers feel most expensive because the visitor is already near the finish line.

That is why pricing questions should be short, direct, and grounded. “Growth is $29 per month” is stronger than “We have flexible plans for teams of all sizes.” Likewise, “Click Start Free in the header” is better than “You can get started from the site.” High-intent questions reward precision.

The chatbot should remove obstacles, not just repeat information

A good website chatbot does not need to close the sale in one message. It only needs to remove the next obstacle. Maybe the obstacle is uncertainty about plan fit. Maybe it is confusion about where to click. Maybe it is hesitation about trust. Every time the site removes one real obstacle, the visitor gets closer to action.

This is a better way to think about website chat. Instead of asking whether the bot can answer everything, ask whether it can answer the right question well enough to keep the conversation moving. If it can do that consistently, it becomes a meaningful part of the conversion path rather than a decorative widget.

What this means for your website

If you are building or evaluating a website chatbot, start by mapping the real question patterns your visitors already ask. Focus on pricing, product fit, onboarding, support, trust, and navigation. Those are the moments that shape conversion most. Then judge the chatbot on whether it answers each category in a way that feels useful for the decision happening behind the question.
