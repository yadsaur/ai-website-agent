The buyer questions that decide whether your chatbot is actually useful

You do not need a chatbot that can talk about everything. You need one that handles the specific questions visitors ask right before they convert or leave.

Start with intent, not volume

Teams often over-focus on broad FAQ coverage and under-focus on conversion intent. A question like “what does this do?” matters. But a question like “how much does it cost?” usually matters more because it appears later in the buying journey.

The cleanest way to design chatbot coverage is to group questions into a few practical buckets: understanding, pricing, trust, technical fit, support, and getting started.

The high-value categories

  • Pricing: cost, plan differences, billing, trials.
  • Product understanding: what it does, who it is for, how setup works.
  • Trust: legitimacy, proof, uptime, real customer signals.
  • Technical fit: integrations, compatibility, embed path.
  • Getting started: where to click, what happens next, how to begin.

Why order matters

Someone asking about pricing is rarely looking for a long product tour. Someone asking where to click is not looking for brand storytelling. Good chatbot UX means matching the answer style to the intent behind the question.

This is why short, direct, grounded replies outperform long generic explanations. The job is not to sound clever. The job is to remove one piece of uncertainty fast.

The practical test

If you want to evaluate your chatbot honestly, ask it ten hard questions in a row. Try cost, setup, plan comparison, support, compatibility, and navigation. If the answers stay clear and grounded, the chatbot is probably doing the right job. If it starts sounding vague, your content or retrieval path still needs work.
